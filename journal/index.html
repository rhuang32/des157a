Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Misusing Lightboxes

Published on January 29, 2024 • 3 min read

Overview

After reading The Nielsen Norman Group's analysis of “Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Misusing LightBoxes” highlights a critical tension in modern web design that I hadn't previously considered fully. While overlays can serve legitimate purposes, such as maintaining context or displaying urgent notifications, their widespread misuse has created significant usability problems that often outweigh their benefits. What particularly struck me was how seemingly minor design decisions, like the placement of close buttons or scroll bars, can have major implications for user accessibility and task completion.

From framework to action

The article's systematic approach using the "5 W's" framework (Who, What, When, Where, and Why) provides an excellent decision-making structure for determining whether an overlay is truly necessary. This resonated strongly with me because it shifts the conversation from "How can we implement this overlay?" to "Should we implement this overlay at all?" I found myself reflecting on various websites I regularly use, noting how many of them employ overlays in ways that actively hinder rather than help the user experience - particularly those frustrating newsletter subscription popups that appear just as I'm trying to read an article.

Takeaway

As a beginner front-end developer, this article has fundamentally changed how I'll approach overlay implementation in future projects. Rather than defaulting to overlays as a convenient solution for displaying additional content, I'll now carefully evaluate whether the same information could be better presented within the main page structure. The article's emphasis on mobile responsiveness and accessibility considerations has also heightened my awareness of how design choices that work well on desktop can create significant barriers for users on different devices or those using screen magnifiers.

Best practices for form design

Published on January 19, 2024 • 5 min read

Ethics about form design

Reading "Best practices for form design" gave me useful tips on making forms that put users first. The article stresses keeping things simple using clear labels, and having a logical order in form design. These methods cut down on mental effort and make it easy for people to use forms leading to happier users. One idea that stuck with me was using inline checks to give quick feedback stopping users from sending in forms with missing or wrong info. This fits with the goal of cutting down on user frustration while making forms easier to use.

Duolingo's sign up flow

A sign-up process that stuck with me was the one on Duolingo. It's simple, straightforward, and splits everything into bite-sized doable steps. As I went through it, the progress bars made me feel like I was getting somewhere, and the instant checks for stuff like email and password made the whole thing feel smooth and easy.

Why it matters for us designers

The article made me realize how significant empathizing with your users is in the short and long run. Forms are ubiquitous, and when designed effectively, they can truly enhance a user's experience all throughout. This article definitely motivated me to approach my form designs with a more thoughtful perspective—focusing approach.
